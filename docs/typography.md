# Typography Reference

Operational reference for identifying typefaces from images and recommending typefaces from briefs.

---

## 1. Anatomy

Identify these parts in any sample. Their treatment is the primary signal of classification, era, and family.

### Vertical metrics

| Term | Definition | Identification cue |
|---|---|---|
| Baseline | Invisible line letters sit on | Bottom of `H`, `x`, `o` (minus overshoot) |
| Cap height | Top of capitals | Top of flat-topped capitals like `H`, `E` |
| Mean line | Top of lowercase x-height | Top of `x`, `n`, `o` |
| x-height | Distance from baseline to mean line | Compare height of `x` to height of `H` |
| Ascender height | Top of `b`, `d`, `h`, `k`, `l`, `f` | Often equal to or above cap height |
| Descender depth | Bottom of `g`, `j`, `p`, `q`, `y` | Compare depth across faces — Garaldes deep, grotesques shallow |
| Overshoot | Curved letters extend slightly past baselines/cap heights | Compare bottom of `O` to bottom of `H` |

x-height ratios:

- Low x-height: classical Garalde proportions (Bembo, Garamond). Ascenders dominate; reads elegant, traditional.
- Medium x-height: most contemporary text faces (Sabon, Times).
- High x-height: modern legibility-oriented faces (Helvetica, Inter, Söhne, Aktiv). Reads functional, contemporary.
- Very high x-height: Druk, condensed display faces, many UI sans (Roobert).

### Stroke parts

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Stem | Main vertical (or main diagonal) stroke. The trunk of `I`, `H`, `L`. |
| Bar / crossbar | Horizontal stroke that crosses or connects, e.g. crossbar of `H`, `e`, `A`, `t`. |
| Arm | Horizontal or upward-diagonal stroke open at one end, e.g. top of `T`, `E`, `K`. |
| Leg | Downward-diagonal stroke, e.g. lower stroke of `K`, `R`. |
| Shoulder | Curved stroke leaving a stem, e.g. top of `n`, `m`, `h`. Its curve and joint shape are highly distinctive. |
| Bowl | Closed curved stroke enclosing a counter, e.g. `b`, `d`, `o`, `p`, `q`, upper of `B`. |
| Counter | Enclosed white space inside `o`, `d`, `p`, `e`, `B`. |
| Aperture | Opening of partly-closed letters: `c`, `e`, `s`, `a`, `n`. Open vs closed aperture is one of the strongest classification signals. |
| Eye | Closed counter of lowercase `e`. |
| Loop / lobe | Lower closed (or open) part of double-storey `g`. |
| Link | Connector between upper bowl and lower loop of double-storey `g`. |
| Ear | Small projecting stroke from upper-right of double-storey `g`, or from `r`. |
| Tail | Descending stroke of `Q`, `K`, `R`, `j`, `y`. |
| Spur | Small projection where a curve meets a stem, often on `G`, `b`. |
| Beak | Sharp projection at the terminal of strokes in some serifs (e.g. arm of `E`, `F`, `T`, `L`). |
| Finial | Tapered or curved end of a stroke that has no serif (often on `e`, `c`, `a`). |
| Terminal | Generic end-of-stroke shape: ball, teardrop, sheared, slab, wedge, square, none. |
| Spine | Central curve of `S` and `s`. Its tilt and modulation reveal stress axis. |
| Apex | Point at which two diagonals meet, top of `A`, `M`, `W`. |
| Vertex | Point at which two diagonals meet at the bottom, base of `V`, `W`, bottom of `M`. |
| Joint | Where curve meets stem (e.g. inside top of `n`, bottom of `u`). Sharp vs soft joints are diagnostic. |

### Stress, axis, contrast

- **Axis / stress**: imaginary line through the thinnest points of a curved stroke (e.g. the thin bits at top and bottom of `O`). 
  - Vertical (rational) axis: thin points at 12 and 6 o'clock. Indicates Transitional, Didone, most sans.
  - Diagonal (humanist) axis: thin points tilted, often around 8 and 2 o'clock. Indicates Humanist and Garalde serifs, broad-nib calligraphic origin.
  - Reverse-stress (Italian / French Clarendon): thicks at top and bottom, thins on sides. Used in display/Western faces.
- **Contrast**: ratio of thick to thin stroke widths.
  - Low / monolinear: grotesques, geometric sans, slabs.
  - Medium: Garalde, Transitional, humanist sans (Gill, Frutiger).
  - High: Didone (Bodoni, Didot), Modernist serifs.
  - Reverse: thins are horizontal, thicks vertical (rare).

### Italic and oblique

- **True italic**: redrawn cursive forms with different construction. Look for single-storey `a`, looped `f`, often `e` with slanted bar, narrower set, calligraphic entry/exit strokes.
- **Oblique**: roman slanted mechanically. Same letterforms, just tilted. Standard for grotesques and geometric sans (Helvetica, Futura).
- **Sloped roman**: between the two — letterforms slightly redrawn but not cursive (Frutiger, many humanist sans).

## 2. Classification Systems

### Vox-ATypI (with modern subdivisions)

#### Humanist (Venetian) serifs

- Era: 1460s–1490s, revived continuously.
- Features: low contrast; strongly diagonal stress; sloped bar on lowercase `e`; chunky bracketed serifs; calligraphic feel.
- Mood: warm, traditional, organic, scholarly.
- Use: literary, museum, heritage, long-form text where warmth matters.
- Exemplars: Centaur, Jenson, Cloister, Adobe Jenson, Hightower, Kepler.

#### Garalde (Old-style)

- Era: 1495 (Aldus/Griffo) through 1700s.
- Features: low-medium contrast; diagonal stress; bracketed serifs; horizontal `e` bar (unlike Humanist); small x-height; long extenders.
- Mood: classical, literary, authoritative without coldness.
- Use: book interiors, heritage editorial.
- Exemplars: Bembo, Garamond, Sabon, Plantin, Granjon, Caslon (often grouped here despite later date), Adobe Caslon, Mrs Eaves.

#### Transitional

- Era: 1700s (Baskerville, Fournier).
- Features: medium-to-high contrast; near-vertical stress; sharper, more refined bracketed serifs; rational construction.
- Mood: rational, clean, slightly formal, English Enlightenment.
- Use: serious editorial, government, finance, classical branding.
- Exemplars: Baskerville, Mrs Eaves XL, Fournier, Bulmer, Times New Roman (functionally transitional), Lyon (modern), Tiempos (modern).

#### Didone (Modern)

- Era: 1780s onward (Bodoni, Didot).
- Features: extreme thick/thin contrast; perfectly vertical stress; unbracketed hairline serifs; flat ball terminals; small x-height typically.
- Mood: luxury, fashion, editorial, neo-classical, brittle elegance.
- Use: fashion mastheads (Vogue, Harper's), perfume, jewellery, high-end editorial.
- Exemplars: Bodoni, Didot, Walbaum, Modern No. 20, GT Sectra (transitional/Didone hybrid), Reckless, Migra, Editorial New, PP Editorial.

#### Mechanistic / Slab Serif

- Era: 1815 onward; Industrial Revolution display origin.
- Subgroups:
  - **Egyptian / geometric slab**: monolinear, unbracketed, square slabs (Rockwell, Memphis, Archer when geometric).
  - **Clarendon / bracketed slab**: bracketed serifs, slight contrast (Clarendon, Sentinel, Caecilia).
  - **Typewriter slab**: monospaced (Courier).
  - **Humanist slab**: slabs over humanist skeleton (PMN Caecilia, FF Meta Serif, Freight Text Slab).
- Mood: sturdy, honest, American, editorial, friendly when rounded.
- Use: headlines, branding wanting weight without coldness, signage, magazines.
- Exemplars: Clarendon, Rockwell, Archer, Sentinel, Caecilia, Roboto Slab, Soleil Slab.

#### Lineal (Sans-serif) — four subgroups

##### Grotesque (19th-century sans)

- Era: 1830s–1900s.
- Features: slightly irregular construction; squarish curves; some contrast in strokes; double-storey `g`; spurred `G`; often a slight flare.
- Mood: industrial, characterful, vernacular, slightly utilitarian.
- Use: editorial, brand systems wanting character without quirk.
- Exemplars: Akzidenz-Grotesk, Monotype Grotesque, Founders Grotesk, Aktiv Grotesk (sits between groups), Theinhardt.

##### Neo-grotesque

- Era: 1950s Swiss.
- Features: even stroke; tight apertures (closed `a`, `c`, `e`, `s`); high x-height; horizontal terminals on `c`, `e`, `s`; oblique not true italic; squarish overall.
- Mood: neutral, Swiss, corporate, rational, often perceived as trustworthy/boring.
- Use: signage, corporate identity, design-led brands wanting neutrality.
- Exemplars: Helvetica, Neue Haas Grotesk, Univers, Söhne (closer to grotesque-neo hybrid), ABC Diatype, Inter (neo-grot-influenced humanist hybrid), Suisse Int'l.

##### Geometric sans

- Era: 1920s Bauhaus onward.
- Features: circular `o`; strokes constructed from circles, squares, triangles; single-storey `a` often; minimal contrast; pointed apex on `A` and `M`.
- Mood: modernist, clean, futuristic, often cold; warmer when humanised (Avenir).
- Use: tech branding, fashion, headlines.
- Exemplars: Futura, Avenir, Avant Garde, Cera, Brandon Grotesque, Gotham (geometric-humanist hybrid), Sofia Pro, Visby, Circular.

##### Humanist sans

- Era: 1916 (Johnston) onward; flourished post-1980s.
- Features: proportions and stroke modulation derived from Roman/Garalde; double-storey `a` and `g` typically; open apertures; some stroke contrast; calligraphic finishes.
- Mood: warm, approachable, contemporary, readable.
- Use: long-form digital reading, wayfinding, healthcare, education, modern brand systems wanting warmth.
- Exemplars: Gill Sans, Johnston, Frutiger, Myriad, Whitney, FF Meta, Fira Sans, Source Sans, Lato, Open Sans, Calibri.

#### Glyphic (Incised)

- Era: capitals based on stone-cut Roman; modern revivals.
- Features: small flared serifs or none; chiselled junctions; often capitals-only or capitals-dominant; stress derived from incision rather than calligraphy.
- Mood: monumental, civic, classical, ceremonial.
- Use: museums, plaques, classical luxury.
- Exemplars: Trajan, Albertus, Friz Quadrata, Optima (glyphic/humanist hybrid), Copperplate Gothic.

#### Script

- Subgroups:
  - **Formal script**: copperplate-derived, high-contrast, joined (Snell Roundhand, Künstler Script).
  - **Casual script**: brush or pen, less formal (Mistral, Brush Script).
  - **Calligraphic**: broad-nib derived (Zapfino).
  - **Handwriting**: imitates everyday writing.
- Mood: formal = wedding, perfume, luxury; casual = friendly, vintage, informal.
- Use: invitations, beauty, hospitality, accents.
- Warnings: rarely suitable for body; never set in all caps.

#### Graphic / Display

- Features: idiosyncratic, stylised, often single-purpose. Anything that does not fit elsewhere.
- Mood: depends entirely on the face.
- Exemplars: Druk (compressed display), Cooper Black, Lobster, Comic Sans (quasi-script).

#### Blackletter

- Subgroups: Textura (rigid, vertical), Rotunda (rounder), Schwabacher, Fraktur.
- Mood: medieval, Germanic, ecclesiastical, metal/heavy-music, traditional newspaper masthead.
- Use: mastheads (NYT), tattoos, music genres, certificates.
- Exemplars: Fette Fraktur, Cloister Black, Goudy Text.

#### Gaelic

- Insular script for Irish typesetting; uncial-influenced; rarely used outside cultural contexts.

#### Non-Latin

- Treat as separate systems. Match weight, x-height, and contrast to companion Latin face. Note that what reads "neutral" or "warm" varies by script.

### Modern subdivisions and overlapping terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Grotesk vs Grotesque vs Neo-grotesque | "Grotesque" = 19th-c. (Akzidenz, Monotype Grot). "Grotesk" is German for sans, often used for revivals (Founders Grotesk, Aktiv Grotesk). "Neo-grotesque" = 1950s Swiss refinement (Helvetica). Lines blur — Söhne, GT America span both. |
| Geometric humanist | Geometric skeleton with humanist warmth: Avenir, Gotham, Proxima Nova. Single-storey `a` rare; proportions less rigid than Futura. |
| Monolinear sans | Strokes uniform width, no modulation. Most geometric sans, many neo-grots. |
| Post-modern serif | Late-20th/21st-century serifs with deliberate quirk: chunky terminals, exaggerated contrast, sharp cuts. Reckless, Migra, GT Super, Editorial New. |
| Pragmatic serif | Contemporary text serif designed for screen and print neutrality: Tiempos, Lyon, Source Serif. Transitional structure, robust on screen. |
| Modern grotesque | 2010s revival merging grotesque character with neo-grot precision: Söhne, GT America, ABC Diatype, Neue Haas Unica. |
| Neue grotesk / Swiss revival | Reissues and refinements of 50s grotesques: Neue Haas Grotesk (Helvetica's reissue), Aktiv Grotesk. |
| Display serif (high contrast) | Didone or Didone-influenced for headlines: Migra, Reckless, GT Super Display, Domaine Display. |
| Editorial serif | Broader category for magazine display: includes pragmatic transitionals (Tiempos Headline) and post-modern Didones. |
| Variable / superfamily | One family with sans, serif, mono, display siblings: Söhne system, GT America, Söhne + Söhne Schmal + Söhne Breit + Söhne Mono. Useful for brand systems. |

## 3. Optical Features

Compare these dimensions when looking at any sample.

### x-height

- Low: ~40-45% of cap height. Garamond, Bembo, classical Garaldes.
- Medium: ~50-55%. Most book serifs, Times.
- High: ~60-70%. Helvetica, Söhne, Aktiv, Inter, Druk.
- Very high: ~70%+. UI fonts, condensed displays.

Higher x-height reads as more contemporary, more functional, more legible at small sizes; lower reads as more classical, more elegant.

### Contrast

| Level | Ratio | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Monolinear | 1:1 | Futura, Helvetica, Rockwell |
| Low | 1:1.2 | Garamond, Frutiger |
| Medium | 1:1.5 | Baskerville, Sabon |
| High | 1:3+ | Bodoni, Didot |
| Extreme | 1:5+ | Display Didones (Migra, Reckless Heavy) |
| Reverse | thicks horizontal | Italian / French Clarendon |

### Stress axis

- Vertical: Didone, Transitional, most sans, most slabs.
- Diagonal (8 to 2 o'clock): Humanist serif, Garalde.
- Diagonal (steep, ~10 to 4): Late Humanist.
- None / mechanical: monolinear sans and slabs.

To check: look at the `O`, identify thinnest points, draw a line through them.

### Terminal style

| Style | Description | Found in |
|---|---|---|
| Ball | Round dot at end of stroke | Garalde and Didone serifs (`a`, `c`, `f`, `r`, `y`). Bodoni, Didot, Caslon. |
| Teardrop | Asymmetric ball | Many Garaldes; e.g. `a` of Caslon. |
| Sheared | Cut at angle | Humanist sans (`c`, `e`, `s` of Frutiger), some grotesques. |
| Horizontal cut | Flat horizontal end | Neo-grotesques (Helvetica `c`, `e`, `s`). |
| Slab | Rectangular | Slab serifs. |
| Wedge | Triangular | Glyphic faces, Albertus, Optima. |
| Hooked | Curved finish | Scripts, calligraphic faces. |
| None / blunt | Stroke ends without flourish | Most geometric sans. |

### Aperture

- Open (large opening on `c`, `e`, `s`, `a`): humanist sans, humanist serif, signage faces (Frutiger, Whitney, Inter). Reads warm and legible.
- Closed (curls inward): neo-grotesques (Helvetica, Akzidenz). Reads neutral, Swiss, designed.
- Medium: most book serifs.

Aperture is one of the fastest ways to distinguish Helvetica (closed) from Aktiv/Söhne/Inter (more open).

### Joints

- Sharp / inkwell: bracket between stem and curve carved out, often in older revivals (Mrs Eaves, Hoefler Text).
- Soft / smooth: typical of contemporary digital faces.
- Sharp angular: post-modern serifs (Reckless, GT Sectra).
- Pen-like: humanist sans where stroke thins where curve enters stem.

### Stroke modulation

- Modulated: curves thicken and thin as a pen would; humanist construction.
- Monolinear: width constant; geometric or industrial.
- Modulated-but-subtle: most neo-grots have slight modulation at joints (the "Helvetica thinning") that reads as monolinear at small sizes.

### Character width and proportion

- Condensed / narrow: Druk, Bebas, condensed cuts.
- Narrow: many display Didones, Tungsten.
- Normal: most text faces.
- Wide / extended: GT America Extended, Druk Wide. Reads bold, confident, Americana.

Roman-derived proportions: capitals like `E`, `F`, `L`, `B`, `S`, `P`, `K` are narrower than `O`, `H`, `N`. Modernist sans (Futura, Helvetica) often equalise widths — a signature of 20th-century construction.

### Italic style

| Style | Marker | Family |
|---|---|---|
| True italic | Single-storey `a`, looped `f`, narrower set, redrawn forms | Most serifs, humanist sans |
| Oblique | Roman tilted, no redrawing | Helvetica, Futura, neo-grotesques, geometric sans |
| Sloped roman | Slight redrawing, no full cursive forms | Gill Sans, Frutiger, some humanist sans |

If `a` becomes single-storey in italic, it's a true italic. If it stays double-storey and just leans, it's an oblique.

### Spacing and fit

- Tight: 1960s-70s phototype influence, display contexts.
- Normal: most contemporary types come tight by default for screen.
- Loose: traditional book setting, wide-set Bodoni revivals.

### Overshoot

Curves and pointed letters extend slightly past base/cap heights to look optically aligned. Heavy display faces have larger overshoot. Monolinear constructed faces (some geometric sans) sometimes lack it, looking subtly off.

## 5. Pairing Principles

### The standard model

Most brand systems need three roles:

1. **Display** — for headlines and brand expression. Can carry character.
2. **Text** — for body copy. Must be neutral and legible at small sizes.
3. **Mono** — for code, numbers, technical details. Sometimes also UI labels.

Some brands collapse display + text into one family (a superfamily like Söhne) and add a contrasting display only for hero usage.

### Contrast principles

- Pair across category, not within: serif + sans, display + text. Two grotesques rarely work.
- Pair similar feeling, different family: a warm Garalde with a warm humanist sans; a sharp post-modern serif with a sharp neo-grotesque.
- Match x-height roughly. If text serif has low x-height and sans has high, body and pulled quotes will jar.
- Match weight and contrast in headline + body: a hairline Didone + a heavy grotesque body is loud but coherent if intentional.

### Common pairings (well-tested)

| Display | Text | Mono | Mood |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tiempos Headline | Söhne | Söhne Mono | Editorial-modern, premium |
| GT Sectra | GT America | GT America Mono | Sharp, contemporary, editorial |
| Reckless | Söhne | JetBrains Mono | Post-modern editorial |
| Domaine Display | Söhne / Aktiv | – | Luxury editorial |
| Druk | Inter | – | Magazine, bold |
| Editorial New | Neue Haas Grotesk | – | Sharp, fashion-leaning |
| Fraunces | Inter | JetBrains Mono | Open-source warm-editorial |
| Migra | GT America | – | High-fashion editorial |
| Caslon | Akzidenz | – | Classical-modernist (Vignelli school) |
| Garamond | Futura | – | Heritage-modern |
| Gotham | Mercury | – | American editorial (Hoefler classic) |
| Whitney | Mercury | Operator Mono | Newsletter / editorial |
| ABC Diatype | ABC Marfa / Reckless | ABC Diatype Mono | Contemporary studio |
| Söhne Breit | Söhne | Söhne Mono | Strong contemporary brand |
| Suisse Works | Suisse Int'l | Suisse Int'l Mono | Swiss-contemporary |
| Roobert | Inter | JetBrains Mono | Tech / startup |
| Avenir | Avenir | – | Single-family, geometric-humanist neutral |
| Mrs Eaves | Mr Eaves Sans | – | Sister-family, literary |

### Within-system superfamilies (single-family pairings)

- Söhne family: Buch (book), Halbfett, Kräftig, Schmal (condensed), Breit (extended), Mono.
- GT America: Standard, Extended, Condensed, Compressed, Mono.
- IBM Plex: Sans, Serif, Mono, Sans Condensed.
- Source: Sans, Serif, Code (Pro).
- Roboto: Sans, Serif, Slab, Mono, Condensed, Flex.
- Inter + Inter Display.
- Fraunces (variable: optical size, soft, wonk axes).

### Warnings

- Don't pair two faces with similar but mismatched x-heights — looks like a mistake, not a choice.
- Don't pair two display faces. Pick one expressive role.
- Avoid pairing two humanist sans (e.g. Gill + Frutiger) — they fight.
- Avoid pairing two Didones — they all look like Bodoni at small sizes and generic together.
- Helvetica + anything classical (Garamond, Caslon) is a tired combination unless the design rests on its tension.
- Mono fonts in body copy reduce reading speed sharply; reserve for code, captions, technical data.
- Script as body copy fails. Always.
- All-caps with low x-height italic looks broken.
- Mixing geometric sans and Didone tightly (Futura + Bodoni) is harsh — used deliberately in fashion editorial; otherwise too cold.

## 6. Identifying Type from Images

Run through this checklist on any sample.

### Step 1: Serif or sans

- Look at stems of `I`, `H`, `n`. If there are projections at top/bottom, it's a serif (or slab). Else sans.

### Step 2: If serif, what kind

- Check stress on `o` or `O`: vertical = Transitional or Didone or modern slab. Diagonal = Humanist or Garalde.
- Check contrast: low = Humanist/Garalde or slab. High = Didone. Medium = Transitional.
- Check serifs: bracketed = Garalde, Transitional, Clarendon. Unbracketed thin = Didone. Slabs = Mechanistic.
- Check `e` bar: slanted = Humanist (Venetian). Horizontal = Garalde or later.

### Step 3: If sans, what kind

- Check `o` shape: perfect circle = geometric. Squarish = grotesque/neo-grotesque. Slightly oval, modulated = humanist.
- Check `a`: double-storey = grotesque/neo-grot/humanist sans. Single-storey = geometric (Futura) or humanist (Gill, sometimes).
- Check apertures of `c`, `e`, `s`: closed (terminals horizontal) = neo-grotesque (Helvetica). Open (sheared) = humanist or open grotesque.
- Check stroke: monolinear with no modulation = geometric. Slight thinning at joints = neo-grotesque. Visible modulation = humanist.
- Check `G`: spurred (with chin) = grotesque/neo-grot. Spurless = geometric.
- Check `R` leg: straight = geometric. Curved/calligraphic = humanist. Angular kicked = grotesque.

### Step 4: Distinctive letters to inspect

Always look at: `a`, `g`, `e`, `R`, `Q`, `M`, `t`, `y`, `&`, `f`, `?`. These carry the most family-specific design decisions.

### Step 5: Italic style

- If italic, is `a` single- or double-storey?
- Is there cursive entry/exit on `n`, `m`?
- Is `f` looped at descender (true italic with descender) or upright with no descender (oblique)?

### Step 6: Weight and width

- Estimate weight: Hairline / Thin / Light / Regular / Medium / Semibold / Bold / Heavy / Black.
- Estimate width: Compressed / Condensed / Normal / Extended / Wide.

### Step 7: Surface clues

- Old print artefacts: ink traps, slight inktrap notches at joints (Bell Centennial, ABC Whyte Inktrap, Retina) — points to news, performance, or post-modern faces with deliberate inktraps.
- Optical sizing: small text version vs display version differ — heavier, wider for small sizes; sharper, lighter for display.

### Step 8: Cross-check against reference set

Once you have features, narrow to a family and pick the most likely faces. Confirm with two or three glyph signatures.

## 7. Distinctive Letterforms

The fingerprints. If a model can read these, identification accuracy jumps.

### Lowercase `a`

- **Double-storey**: closed bowl + curved upper arm. Standard for serifs, neo-grotesques, humanist sans.
  - Helvetica: tight closed terminal hooks back inward.
  - Akzidenz: similar but more open.
  - Söhne: double-storey, slightly open terminal — between Helvetica and Aktiv.
  - Garamond: ball terminal on top arm; tear-shape bowl.
  - Caslon: similar but heavier ball.
- **Single-storey**: simple bowl with stem. Standard for italics, geometric sans.
  - Futura: pure circle + straight stem.
  - Avenir: slightly modulated single-storey.
  - Gill Sans: single-storey roman (rare for non-geometric sans).

### Lowercase `g`

- **Double-storey (binocular)**: upper bowl, link, lower loop, ear. The most expressive lowercase glyph.
  - Garamond: small upper bowl, large open loop, small ear.
  - Caslon: open loop, prominent ear angled up.
  - Helvetica: closed loop, curt ear.
  - Söhne: double-storey, restrained.
  - Inter: double-storey designed for screen.
  - Whitney: double-storey, soft.
  - Hoefler Text / Mrs Eaves: prominent quirky ear.
- **Single-storey (monocular)**: simple bowl with hook descender. 
  - Futura, Avenir, most geometric sans.
  - Italic forms of most serifs.
  - Frutiger.

The `g` is the single most identifying glyph in many faces.

### Lowercase `e`

- Bar angle: slanted bar = Humanist Venetian (Centaur). Horizontal = everything later.
- Aperture: closed (Helvetica) vs open (Frutiger, Whitney).
- Eye size: small = older Garalde. Large = contemporary text serif.

### Capital `R`

- Leg style:
  - Straight diagonal: most grotesques, geometric sans.
  - Curved leg ending in serif/foot: Garalde and Transitional serifs.
  - Kicked leg with curl: Caslon (curls outward); Garamond has subtler curve.
  - Straight tucked under bowl: Trajan, glyphic forms.
  - Long extended leg: Helvetica's `R` has a slight curl; Akzidenz straighter; Söhne curved.
  - Distinctive "ham" leg with thick joint: Founders Grotesk.

### Capital `Q`

- Tail style:
  - Cross-stroke through circle: Garamond, Caslon (often baroque tail extending right).
  - Subtle curl inside: Helvetica.
  - Long sweeping tail: Bodoni, Didot, GT Sectra (dramatic).
  - Disconnected tail (separate stroke): Trajan, some glyphics.
  - Tail tucked under: Gotham, Avenir.

### Capital `M`

- Splay (whether legs straight or angled):
  - Splayed (legs slope outward): Garalde, Humanist (Garamond, Bembo).
  - Vertical sides (legs straight): Transitional, neo-grotesque, geometric sans.
  - Pointed apex meeting at baseline (vertex on baseline): Centaur, Trajan, many old-style.
  - Apex with small flat top: Helvetica.
- Centre-stroke depth:
  - Reaches baseline: classical Roman, Trajan.
  - Stops above baseline: most contemporary.

### Lowercase `t`

- Top:
  - Flat / cut horizontal: Helvetica, neo-grotesques.
  - Angled / curved top: humanist sans (Frutiger, Gill).
  - Pointed: some geometric sans.
- Bottom curve depth and crossbar position vary by family.

### Lowercase `y`

- Tail:
  - Straight diagonal: most grotesques.
  - Curved hook: humanist sans, serifs.
  - Curled: many Garaldes (Caslon, Garamond).
  - Straight slab: slab serifs, geometric sans.

### Ampersand `&`

- The most varied glyph in any family. Use as a signature.
  - Et-ligature (visible "Et"): Garamond, Caslon (italic versions especially), Trajan-derived.
  - Figure-eight: most contemporary grotesques, Helvetica.
  - Italic flourish: distinct from roman in nearly every serif italic.
  - Look for the `&` in italic — it's often the most decorative glyph in the font.

### Lowercase `f`

- Descender or no descender:
  - Italic `f` with full descender + loop: true italic (most serifs, humanist sans true italics).
  - Upright `f` with hooked top, no descender: roman; oblique italic.
- Crossbar position: high in geometric, mid in most others.

### Question mark `?`

- Curve: simple semicircle (geometric, neo-grot) vs ornate (Garalde, post-modern serif).
- Dot shape: round vs square (neo-grots often have square dots, e.g. Akzidenz; Helvetica round).

### Numerals

- Lining vs old-style figures:
  - Lining figures (all sit on baseline, cap-height): standard for sans, modern serifs.
  - Old-style figures (varying heights, with descenders on 3, 4, 5, 7, 9): Garalde, Transitional book faces.
- `1` style: serif on top + base = traditional; flag at top = grotesque; simple stem = geometric.
- `4` open or closed top: open = Helvetica; closed = many grotesques.
- `7` with foot serif: classical; without: modern.

## 9. Common Briefing Misconceptions

Anticipate these. Reframe them with the client when needed.

- **"Modern" is ambiguous.** Could mean (a) Bauhaus-modernist (Futura, 1920s), (b) Swiss-modernist (Helvetica, 1950s), (c) contemporary (Söhne, GT America, 2010s), or (d) post-modern (Reckless, Migra, 2018+). Confirm by asking for examples.
- **"Clean" rarely means grotesque only.** Often means generous spacing, restraint, lack of decoration — achievable with humanist sans (Inter), geometric (Avenir), or even a quiet serif (Tiempos Text).
- **"Elegant" is multi-directional.** Didone (high-contrast luxury), Transitional (refined English), formal script (wedding), or even a quiet grotesque can all be elegant.
- **"Bold" is not the same as "loud".** A wide grotesque at regular weight can feel bolder than a hairline Didone at black weight.
- **"Premium" is not "luxury".** Premium can be quiet (Söhne Buch). Luxury usually wants high contrast and restraint together.
- **"Tech" doesn't necessarily mean geometric.** Most contemporary tech brands now use modern grotesques (Söhne, Aktiv, Inter), not Futura. Geometric reads slightly dated for AI/SaaS in 2026.
- **"Friendly" is not the same as "rounded".** Open aperture + warm modulation is friendlier than mechanically rounded corners.
- **"Heritage" doesn't always mean serif.** Heritage in beverages/automotive can include grotesques and slabs from same era. Heritage in publishing usually means Garalde.
- **"Minimal" doesn't preclude character.** A single quirky weight at restrained scale can be minimal and characterful.
- **Helvetica is a default, not a brand.** If a client asks for "something like Helvetica," they may want a refined contemporary alternative (Söhne, Aktiv, Neue Haas Grotesk) rather than literal Helvetica.
- **Times is a screen compromise.** "Like Times" may mean Transitional in feeling — Tiempos, Lyon, or Source Serif fit better.
- **"Custom feel" is achievable without custom.** Less-common high-quality faces (ABC Diatype, GT Sectra, Söhne) read as bespoke compared to system defaults.
- **All-caps ≠ formal.** Geometric all-caps can be modernist; small caps in Garalde are scholarly; condensed all-caps are editorial-fashion. Set context.
- **Variable fonts ≠ default tech aesthetic.** Variability is a technology, not an aesthetic. Inter and Fraunces are variable but read entirely differently.
- **"More legible" usually means: open apertures, larger x-height, slight modulation, lower contrast, true italic.** Not just "cleaner".
- **Brand owner says "elegant serif" but cites Helvetica.** Common. They likely mean "refined" rather than literally serif. Ask which examples they react to.

## Cross-reference: family by feature

Use this matrix when triangulating a face from features.

| Feature | Likely families |
|---|---|
| Vertical stress + high contrast + hairline serifs | Didone (Bodoni, Didot, Domaine Display) |
